\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
Diffusion models are generative models that learn to reverse a gradual noising process. Rather than producing an image in a single step, a diffusion model begins with random noise and refines it through a sequence of denoising operations until a coherent sample is obtained. This report develops the material from our presentation slides into a concise technical discussion of the main ideas behind Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) \cite{ho2020ddpm,nichol2021improved}. It outlines the forward and reverse processes, explains the role of the U-Net backbone and timestep conditioning, and presents the standard noise-prediction objective used in training. The report also discusses why diffusion models have become influential in modern generative modeling, particularly because of their stable optimization behavior, strong sample quality, and flexibility in conditional generation \cite{song2019generative,rombach2022latent}. Finally, it notes practical and ethical limitations, including slow sampling, high computational cost, and the need for responsible deployment.

\section{Introduction}
Generative modeling studies how to learn a probability distribution from observed data so that new samples can be drawn from it. In the case of images, the goal is not only to reproduce local texture, but also to preserve larger structure, object relationships, and overall realism. Diffusion-based methods have become especially important in this area because they combine a clear probabilistic interpretation with strong empirical performance \cite{ho2020ddpm,song2020ddim}.

Figure~\ref{fig:motivation-real-vs-ai} illustrates this motivation. The generated face on the right appears plausible even though it does not correspond to a real person. A useful generative model must therefore do more than memorize training examples; it must capture the statistical regularities of the data well enough to synthesize new samples.

The central idea of diffusion modeling is to decompose generation into many small denoising steps. During training, real data are gradually perturbed with Gaussian noise. Because the corruption process is known in advance, the model can be trained to predict the added noise at arbitrary timesteps. During sampling, the learned process is applied in reverse: the model starts from random noise and progressively recovers structure. This formulation turns image generation into a sequence of manageable estimation problems rather than a single difficult prediction.

\subsection{Forward Diffusion}
The forward process is a Markov chain that progressively perturbs a clean sample $\vect{x}_0 \in \R^d$ with Gaussian noise. At each timestep $t$, a small variance term $\beta_t$ determines how much corruption is introduced:
\begin{equation}
    q(\vect{x}_t \mid \vect{x}_{t-1}) =
    \mathcal{N}\!\left(\sqrt{1-\beta_t}\,\vect{x}_{t-1}, \beta_t \vect{I}\right).
    \label{eq:forward-markov}
\end{equation}

If we define $\alpha_t = 1-\beta_t$ and $\alphabar_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t}\alpha_s$, then a sample at any timestep can be written directly as
\begin{equation}
    \vect{x}_t = \sqrt{\alphabar_t}\,\vect{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\alphabar_t}\,\vect{\epsilon},
    \quad \vect{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\vect{0}, \vect{I}).
    \label{eq:closed-form-forward}
\end{equation}

Equation~\ref{eq:closed-form-forward} is convenient during training because it allows the model to sample a noisy state at any timestep directly, without simulating all previous steps.

\subsection{Reverse Diffusion}
Once the data has been corrupted, the generative task becomes the reverse problem: infer how to move from $\vect{x}_t$ back to a less noisy sample $\vect{x}_{t-1}$. The model parameterized by $\theta$ learns a reverse distribution of the form
\begin{equation}
    p_{\theta}(\vect{x}_{t-1} \mid \vect{x}_t) =
    \mathcal{N}\!\left(\vect{\mu}_{\theta}(\vect{x}_t,t), \vect{\Sigma}_{\theta}(\vect{x}_t,t)\right).
    \label{eq:reverse-process}
\end{equation}

In practice, many implementations train a neural network to predict the added noise rather than predict clean pixels directly. This reparameterization simplifies learning because the network only needs to estimate the perturbation that separates $\vect{x}_t$ from the underlying clean sample \cite{ho2020ddpm,nichol2021improved}.

\section{Model Architecture and Training}
In practice, strong performance depends not only on the probabilistic formulation, but also on the neural architecture used to model the reverse process. Most image diffusion systems employ a U-Net-style backbone with multi-scale feature extraction and skip connections \cite{ronneberger2015unet}. The network receives two inputs: a noisy image $\vect{x}_t$ and an embedding of the timestep $t$.

\subsection{Noise-Prediction Objective}
The standard DDPM training objective is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}}
    =
    \E_{\vect{x}_0,t,\vect{\epsilon}}
    \left[
        \left\|
            \vect{\epsilon} - \vect{\epsilon}_{\theta}(\vect{x}_t,t)
        \right\|_2^2
    \right].
    \label{eq:noise-objective}
\end{equation}

This loss is convenient because the target noise $\vect{\epsilon}$ is known exactly; it is sampled by the training procedure itself. As a result, supervision is explicit and comparatively stable. Instead of learning to generate a final image in one pass, the network solves many smaller denoising tasks.

\subsection{Training Workflow}
The complete training routine can be summarized as Algorithm~\ref{alg:diffusion-training}. The procedure alternates between sampling a real image, corrupting it to a randomly chosen timestep, and updating the network so that its predicted noise matches the actual perturbation.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Training a diffusion model with noise prediction}
\label{alg:diffusion-training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Training dataset $\mathcal{D}$, noise schedule $\{\beta_t\}_{t=1}^{T}$, neural network $\vect{\epsilon}_{\theta}$
\While{training has not converged}
    \State Sample a clean image $\vect{x}_0 \sim \mathcal{D}$
    \State Sample a timestep $t$ uniformly from $\{1,\dots,T\}$
    \State Sample Gaussian noise $\vect{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\vect{0},\vect{I})$
    \State Form a noisy input using Equation~\ref{eq:closed-form-forward}
    \State Predict $\hat{\vect{\epsilon}} = \vect{\epsilon}_{\theta}(\vect{x}_t,t)$
    \State Compute $\mathcal{L}_{\text{simple}} = \|\vect{\epsilon} - \hat{\vect{\epsilon}}\|_2^2$
    \State Update $\theta$ using gradient descent
\EndWhile
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Why Diffusion Models Perform Well}
Diffusion models perform well in practice for several related reasons. First, the training target is available analytically once Gaussian noise has been sampled, which helps avoid the adversarial instability often associated with GAN-based training. Second, iterative denoising allows coarse structure to emerge before fine details are refined. Third, conditioning information such as text, class labels, or masks can be incorporated naturally into the denoising network, which makes the framework adaptable to many tasks.

These characteristics help explain why diffusion systems became competitive with, and in many settings preferable to, earlier generative approaches. Table~\ref{tab:model-comparison} gives a concise comparison that highlights common trade-offs.

Beyond creative media, diffusion models are increasingly relevant in technical domains. In medical imaging, researchers use them for denoising, reconstruction, and data augmentation. In scientific design, related models are being explored for molecule generation and inverse problems. Latent diffusion models further reduce the cost of high-resolution synthesis by performing denoising in a compressed latent space rather than directly in pixel space \cite{rombach2022latent}.

\section{Limitations and Responsible Use}
Despite their strengths, diffusion models still have important limitations. The first is \textbf{slow sampling}: high-quality generation may require dozens to hundreds of denoising steps, which increases latency. The second is \textbf{high computational cost}: training large systems demands substantial GPU memory, long training times, and significant electrical power. The third is \textbf{system complexity}: practical deployments often rely on careful prompt conditioning, safety filters, and post-processing pipelines.

There are also broader ethical concerns. Diffusion models can reproduce harmful biases present in their training data, imitate artistic styles without consent, or be used to create misleading synthetic media. For this reason, technical progress should be considered alongside dataset transparency, usage policies, and clear attribution practices.

\section{Conclusion}
Diffusion models offer a clear and influential framework for modern generative modeling. By learning to reverse a controlled corruption process, they connect probabilistic reasoning, neural network design, and iterative optimization in a way that is both mathematically grounded and practically effective. This combination explains their strong performance across image generation and related tasks.

At the same time, the topic becomes much easier to understand when its components are presented in a clear sequence: forward diffusion, reverse diffusion, architecture, training objective, applications, and limitations. A structured presentation of these ideas shows not only how diffusion models generate realistic samples, but also why they remain an important subject in contemporary machine learning.
